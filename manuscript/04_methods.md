# Methods

## Setup and notation

Within a single commuting-zone window $W$ (a polygon, projected to local UTM),
let $X = \{x_1,\dots,x_n\}$ be the open hospital locations falling in $W$. Let
$p(u)$ be the population surface (LandScan ambient, or GPW residential)
evaluated at location $u \in W$. We ask whether $X$ is consistent with a point
process whose first-order intensity is proportional to population.

## Zone inclusion criterion

The inhomogeneous K-function is a second-order (pairwise) summary, so it requires
enough hospitals per zone to form enough point pairs to estimate. Below a small
count the Monte-Carlo envelope is degenerate and the test has no power. We
therefore compute the test only for zones with at least **8 open hospitals**;
the threshold is a parameter (`min_hospitals`) and we report a sensitivity check
at higher values. Of the 597 commuting zones, **261 meet the threshold and 336
do not**, but the excluded zones contain only **8.9% of ambient population**, so
the analysis still covers about **91% of where Americans are during the day**.
Excluded zones are predominantly low-population (12 contain no hospital at all)
and are reported in full in the supplement. Skipping the clustering test in a
sparse zone is not the same as ignoring its access: "is supply clustered relative
to population within the zone" is not well posed with a few points, whereas the
relevant question there, how far the population sits from the nearest hospital,
is addressed by the complementary population-weighted coverage metric rather than
by $K_{\text{inhom}}$.

## The population-proportional intensity

We define the null intensity as population, rescaled so its integral over the
window equals the observed hospital count:

$$
\lambda(u) \;=\; n \,\frac{p(u)}{\int_W p(v)\,dv}, \qquad u \in W .
$$

By construction $\int_W \lambda(u)\,du = n$, so a process drawn from $\lambda$
produces $n$ hospitals in expectation and the inhomogeneous K-function below is
directly comparable to its theoretical Poisson value $\pi r^2$. Because
$\lambda$ is supplied by the raster, there is no kernel bandwidth to choose and
no leave-one-out intensity to floor, so the instability class that arises when
$\lambda$ is estimated from sparse points does not occur. A small positive floor
is applied only to raster cells with zero ambient population that nonetheless
contain a hospital (for example, an industrial-zoned cell), to keep $1/\lambda$
finite; its level is reported and varied as a sensitivity check.

## The inhomogeneous K-function

We use the inhomogeneous K-function [@baddeley_moller_waagepetersen_2000], the
second-order summary reweighted by the (here, externally supplied) intensity:

$$
\hat{K}_{\text{inhom}}(r) \;=\; \frac{1}{|W|}
\sum_{i}\sum_{j \neq i}
\frac{\mathbf{1}\{\lVert x_i - x_j \rVert \le r\}\, e(x_i, x_j)}
     {\lambda(x_i)\,\lambda(x_j)} ,
$$

with $e(\cdot,\cdot)$ the Ripley isotropic edge-correction weight. Under the
null that hospitals follow population, $K_{\text{inhom}}(r) \approx \pi r^2$.
Values above $\pi r^2$ indicate that hospitals are more clustered than
population warrants at scale $r$ (over-concentration); values below indicate a
more regular-than-population arrangement.

## The null and the Monte-Carlo envelope

The reference distribution is generated by simulating hospital patterns from the
population-proportional inhomogeneous Poisson process, $\text{rpoispp}(\lambda)$,
and recomputing $\hat{K}_{\text{inhom}}$ for each. With `nsim` simulations the
pointwise envelope is the simulation-wise min/max band (matching `spatstat`'s
default `nrank = 1`). A radius $r$ is flagged as over-concentrated where the
observed curve exceeds the upper envelope and as dispersed where it falls below
the lower envelope. Default parameters are `nsim = 999`, an $r$ grid in meters
appropriate to metro scale, Ripley isotropic edge correction, and a fixed
`seed` for reproducibility, with independent L'Ecuyer-CMRG streams across
simulations.

The first-order intensity $\lambda$ is identical in the observed statistic and
in every simulation, because it is the population surface and is not re-estimated
per simulation. The test therefore conditions cleanly on population and isolates
the second-order (clustering) question.

## Global (family-wise) envelope test across commuting zones

Running a pointwise envelope in each of several hundred commuting zones invites
multiple-comparison inflation. We therefore apply a global envelope test (the
Myllymäki et al. extreme-rank-length, ERL, construction)
[@myllymaki_etal_2017] so that a zone's significant verdict controls the
family-wise error rate over the $r$ grid, and we report the distribution of
global $p$-values across zones.

## Local decomposition (where the deviation is)

The global $K_{\text{inhom}}$ is an average over the window; it states that a
commuting zone departs from proportionality, not where. We decompose it with the
local inhomogeneous K-function, which yields one local contribution per hospital
and, on the complementary side, a population-weighted coverage map of where
people sit far from any hospital relative to expectation. This converts a global
verdict into an interpretable map of over-served cores and under-served gaps.

## Contrast design (ambient versus residential)

Every quantity above is computed twice, once with the LandScan ambient
$\lambda$ and once with the GPW residential $\lambda$, holding the hospital
pattern, window, edge correction, $r$ grid, and seeds fixed. The reported
contrast is the set of commuting zones whose verdict differs between the two
demand surfaces.

## Implementation and reproducibility

The reference engine is R / `spatstat` (`Kinhom`, `envelope`, `rpoispp` on a
pixel-image intensity, `localKinhom`, and the `GET` package for the global
test). An independent Python port (`numpy`, `scipy`, `shapely`, `geopandas`,
adapted from the validated Kcross port) serves as a cross-check. Each run writes
the envelope curve, a verdict, and a JSON metadata record (counts, parameters,
projection, runtime). Code is in `code/` and is not part of the submitted
manuscript.
